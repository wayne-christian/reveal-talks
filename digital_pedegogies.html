From Crit to Capture

Using Video and AI to Rethink Feedback at Scale

I’m going to share a feedback process we’ve been developing in BA Graphic Design. This is not a research argument, but a practical workflow built around recorded critique, transcription, and AI-supported summaries. The focus is on how it works in practice and what colleagues might take from it.

Wayne Christian

Designer, Developer & Senior Lecturer

I’ll move fairly quickly. I’ll begin with the teaching context, then show the shift from critique to capture, briefly share student response, and then spend most of the time on the workflow that supports this at scale.

Context

~60 students per year.
Two modules per semester.
Two staff delivering feedback.
Holistic critique across the module.

Graphic Design typically has around sixty students per year group. Students take two modules per semester, which simplifies scheduling and delivery. Feedback is usually delivered by two staff, rather than three or four, which reduces intensity and improves clarity. Importantly, feedback is holistic, looking across the module rather than isolated tasks.

Designing for how students think

These are design students, so feedback works best when it can be shown as well as described. Video and tools like FigJam allow visual demonstration, and combining video, transcript, and summary gives students multiple ways to engage with feedback. This is also useful for increasingly diverse and neurodiverse cohorts.

Why capture feedback?

Students must listen, interpret, and note-take simultaneously.
Feedback is often not fully actioned afterwards.

Students are often trying to do too much at once. They are listening, interpreting, and making notes at the same time. As a result, they may leave with a general sense of feedback, but not always clear actions they can follow up on.

What didn’t work

Unrecorded sessions relying on student note-taking
led to fragmented and non-actionable feedback.

We trialled a model where sessions were not recorded and students captured their own notes. In practice, this resulted in fragmented understanding. The act of writing often interfered with listening and processing.

From crit to capture

This is the shift. The session is recorded, transcribed, and summarised. The value is not just the AI summary, but the combination of formats that makes the feedback revisitable and easier to act on.

The process

Record → Transcript → Summary
Feedback becomes structured, revisitable, and actionable.

The process is simple. Capture the session, generate a transcript, and create a structured summary. Students can then revisit the feedback in whichever format suits them, written, video, transcription

Existing video feedback (Blackboard)

This is the current Blackboard video feedback interface. It allows recording and provides a transcript, which is useful, but what we are seeing here is a literal transcription of the session. There is no structure, no summary, and no indication of key points or actions for the student. This means the student still has to interpret the feedback themselves, which brings us back to the same issue.

Current workflow

Feedback is currently structured using recordings, transcripts, and AI summaries, but then manually transferred into Blackboard.

At the moment, this process is not fully automated. Feedback is captured, summarised, and structured, but then copied and pasted into Blackboard manually. This works at module level, but it would not be sustainable at scale if implemented across a programme or the wider school. This is really the point where workflow becomes the key issue, rather than pedagogy.

Scaling constraints

Manual workflow, DPIA requirements, and system limitations prevent this from scaling beyond module level

At the moment, the limitation is not the feedback itself, but how it is managed at scale. Feedback still needs to be transferred manually into Blackboard, which is not sustainable across larger cohorts or programmes. There are also institutional constraints, such as DPIA requirements and the absence of voice recognition, which affect automation. So the challenge here is not pedagogy, but workflow and system integration.

Structured feedback

In contrast, this approach takes the same feedback session and produces a structured summary, with clear key points and actions. The difference is not the feedback itself, but how it is interpreted and presented back to the student.

Feedback aligned to learning outcomes

This is the same feedback session, but now structured around the module’s learning outcomes. Instead of a general summary, the feedback is organised into: strengths, areas for development, and clear actions linked directly to assessment criteria. The important point is that the feedback itself hasn’t changed — what has changed is how it is structured and presented back to the student. This makes it much easier to understand and act on.

Designing the output

This shows how that output is configured. The summary format is defined in advance, including the role, the structure, and the learning outcomes. So instead of relying on a generic AI response, we are shaping the output to reflect how the module is assessed. This is where the approach becomes pedagogical rather than just technical.

Efficiency and cost model

£360 per staff licence per year
~£42k projected cost (naïve model)
~50% time saving → ~£21k workload saving
Single admin account → scalable solution

A practical issue here is cost. Copilot for Teams licences are approximately £360 per person per year. If applied across all staff and potentially students, this quickly becomes financially unmanageable. Initial projections suggested assessment costs of around forty-two thousand pounds. However, by rethinking the workflow and using a single admin account linked to meetings, we can still generate transcripts and AI summaries without requiring every participant to have a licence. Combined with a reduction in duplicated feedback writing, this resulted in an indicative 50 percent reduction in workload, equating to approximately twenty-one thousand pounds in savings. This is why the next step is automating the output into Blackboard-ready formats.

Next steps

From automation → to adaptive feedback systems

In the short term, the focus is automation. We need to remove manual copy-and-paste processes to make this viable at scale. Beyond that, we are exploring integration with institutional systems. Longer term, there is an opportunity to move toward more adaptive feedback. Using student profiling or existing data, AI agents could present feedback in ways that better match how individuals engage with information. This could mean adjusting tone, structure, or level of detail, rather than changing the feedback itself. The aim is not just faster feedback, but feedback that is easier for students to understand and act on.

A useful process to share

Extends critique beyond the session Improves clarity and accessibility Reduces repetitive admin work

This is not a finished solution, but a useful process. It extends critique beyond the live moment, improves clarity for students, and begins to reduce repetitive administrative work. The intention is simply to share something others may adapt.
